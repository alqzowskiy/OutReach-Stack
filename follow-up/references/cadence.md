# Follow-up cadence

How long to wait, how many times to reach out, and the rule that makes follow-ups work.

## The one rule
Every follow-up must add a new reason to reply. If the only content is "have you seen my last email", do not send it. A bump with nothing new trains the recipient to ignore your name.

## Timing (cold sequence)
- **Touch 1 (the original):** day 0.
- **Touch 2 (first follow-up):** 3 to 4 business days later. Short, new angle.
- **Touch 3:** about a week after touch 2.
- **Touch 4 (break-up):** 1 to 2 weeks after touch 3, and it is the last one.

Warm threads (they replied once, then went quiet) can move faster and forgive a closer cadence. Cold sequences need the space.

## How many touches
Cap a cold sequence at three or four touches, then stop with a break-up. Past that you are not persistent, you are noise, and you risk a spam complaint. Quality of angle beats quantity of sends.

## New angles to rotate through
- A new proof point or result you can now cite.
- A trigger event in their world: a launch, a raise, a new hire, a press mention, a job change.
- A smaller ask than the original: a yes or no, one question, a referral to the right person.
- A genuinely useful resource, sent with no ask attached.

## Follow-up-specific slop to kill
- "Just circling back", "just following up", "touching base".
- "Bumping this to the top of your inbox."
- "Per my last email", "as mentioned below".
- "I wanted to follow up to see if you had any thoughts."
- Re-pasting the entire first email under "in case you missed this".

Each of these signals a template and adds zero new information. Replace it with a new angle from the list above.

## The break-up message
- Say plainly that this is the last time you will reach out.
- Give an easy way back in ("if the timing changes, just reply and I will pick it up").
- Stay gracious. No guilt, no passive aggression.
- Keep it to two or three sentences. It often gets the highest reply rate of the whole sequence because it removes the pressure.
