# LinkedIn: channel rules

Physical limits of each LinkedIn surface. A draft that breaks them does not get shown.

## Connection request note
- Hard cap around 300 characters. Write one sentence, maybe two.
- No links. They do not render, and they read as spam.
- One idea only: why you want to connect, tied to something specific. Not a pitch.
- A vague note ("would love to connect and explore synergies") is worse than no note. If you have nothing specific to say, sending no note is a valid choice.
- The note's only job is to earn the accept. Save the ask for after they connect.
- Count the characters before you show it. If it runs over 300, cut, do not abbreviate into something cryptic.

## InMail
- A subject line is required and short. Keep it under ten words and make it specific. It drives the open the way an email subject does.
- The body cap is generous (around 1900 characters), but that is a ceiling, not a target. Keep it to a short email's length and front-load the ask.
- An InMail costs a credit and signals you paid to reach them, so do not waste it on a feature dump. One ask, one reason to care.
- Links are allowed, but use at most one, and only if it serves the ask. Several links look automated.
- People read InMail on a phone. Short paragraphs, no walls of text.

## DM (already connected)
- No subject. The first line is the whole open.
- Conversational and short. You already have the connection, so skip the formal reintroduction.
- One ask, stated early. A DM that buries the ask under small talk gets left on read.

## Across all three
- Less formal than email, but not sloppy. Write like a person who respects the reader's time.
- Mobile-first: assume every message is read on a phone screen.
- No "I'd love to add you to my professional network". That is the default LinkedIn template and it signals zero effort.
