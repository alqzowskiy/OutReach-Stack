# Anti-slop checklist

Run every draft through this before showing it to the user. This file is the single source of truth, shared by `cold-email`, `inmail`, and `follow-up`, so the rules apply to every channel.

How to use it: when a line trips a rule, rewrite it, do not just delete it. The goal is a message that reads like one busy person wrote it to another, not a message with the obvious tells filed off.

## AI tells (cut on sight)

- **The em dash.** Never use it. Replace it with a comma, a colon, parentheses, or two sentences. Do not smuggle it back in as a spaced hyphen ( - ) or an en dash. This is the clearest single giveaway that text was generated.
- **The rule-of-three cadence.** "Fast, simple, and reliable." "Build, ship, and scale." Real people rarely speak in tidy triplets. Pick the one word that matters.
- **"Not just X, it's Y" and "not only X but also Y."** A generated rhythm. Say the thing once.
- **Tell words.** delve, leverage (as a verb), robust, seamless, synergy, unlock, elevate, empower, streamline, spearhead, cutting-edge, best-in-class, game-changer, revolutionize, tapestry, testament, underscore, "deep dive", "move the needle", "circle back", "touch base", "low-hanging fruit", "at the end of the day", "needless to say", "it is worth noting", "in today's landscape", "fast-paced world", "ever-evolving", "ecosystem".
- **Hollow openers.** "I hope this email finds you well." "I came across your profile." "I wanted to reach out." "My name is X and I am the founder of Y." None of these earn the open.
- **Overformatting.** Bold on every other phrase, emoji as section markers, or a bullet list inside a 90 word email. A short message is plain paragraphs.
- **Smart punctuation a person typing fast would not produce.** Curly quotes, the ellipsis character, decorative symbols.
- **Suspiciously balanced prose.** Every sentence the same length, every clause mirrored. Human writing is a little uneven, and that is what makes it read as human.

## Fake personalization

- "I loved your recent post" with no specific detail is worse than no compliment, because it signals a template. Name the exact post, number, or decision, or cut the line.
- Merge-field phrasing ("As a [role] at [company], you surely care about...") reads as mail-merge. Be specific about this one person, or be honestly generic and short.
- The test: if the opening sentence could be sent unchanged to fifty other people, it is not personalization.

## Structural sins

- **The ask is buried at the bottom.** Move it into the first third. The reader should know what you want before they decide whether to keep reading.
- **More than one CTA.** Two asks compete and produce zero replies. Cut to one.
- **Sender-first disease.** A paragraph about who you are before the point lands. The reader does not care until they know why you are writing.
- **Feature dump.** Three sentences listing what the product does. Replace it with the one outcome that matters to this person.
- **Hedging stacks.** "I was just wondering if maybe you might possibly have a moment to perhaps..." Say it once, directly.

## The rewrite recipe

When a draft trips the rules, fix it in this order:

1. Delete the first sentence if it is a greeting with no content. Start with the hook or the reason.
2. Move the ask up so it lands by the third sentence.
3. Replace every adjective you can with a number or a concrete noun. "Huge time savings" becomes "about a day a week".
4. Cut the message to one idea and one CTA.
5. Read it aloud. Anything you would not say out loud to this person, cut.

## Micro before and after

- Before: "I hope this email finds you well. I wanted to reach out to explore potential synergies." After: "Saw you just moved on-call to a four-person rotation. We cut ours from a day a week to near zero."
- Before: "Our robust, seamless platform empowers teams to unlock new efficiencies." After: "It deletes the flaky tests that make CI fail at random."
- Before: "I would love to hop on a quick call to discuss how we might be able to help. Let me know your thoughts!" After: "Worth 15 minutes Tuesday or Thursday? If not, I will send the writeup and leave it there."

## Final pass

- Read it as the recipient on a phone. Is the reason you are writing clear in the first two lines? Is the ask one easy action?
- Would a busy person actually reply, or just feel pitched?
- If you would be slightly embarrassed to send it because it sounds like a bot, it does. Rewrite it plainer.
